Managing Equity Market Risk

  • β=covSI/σ2I
  • βTS=βSS+Nfβff
    • Nf=[(βT-βS)/βF](S/f)
Beta measures the relationship between a stock portfolio and the market portfolio (systematic risk). To increase beta, buy futures. To decrease beta, sell futures.

Synthetic Equity Exposure

  • Long stock + short futures = long risk-free bond
  • N*f=V×(1+r)T/(qf)
  • V*=(N*fqf)/(1+r)T
  • Equitizing cash
  • Converting equity to cash
The relationships between a futures or forward contract and the underlying is determined by a a formula that relates the risk-free rate to the dividends on the underlying asset. 'N*f' is the number of futures contracts to be bought/sold. 'V' is the market value of the assets to be invested. 'q' is the multiplier. 'f' is the futures prices. Assuming a given index is a price index, an investor doesn't capture any return attributable to dividends. Equitizing cash refers to taking a cash balance and converting it into an equity position while maintaining the liquidity provided by cash.

Synthetic Equity Exposure (cont'd)

Construct a position of $150MM in a synthetic index fund with a dividend yield of 3.0%. A futures contract on the index is priced at $5,000 with a multiplier of $10. The risk-free rate is 4% and the position will be held for 3 months.

  • Nf=($150MM×1.04.25/($10×5,000)=$3,029.56
  • V*=(3,030×10×5,000)/1.04.25=$150,021,775
  • Stock units effectively purchased = (3,030×10)/1.03.25

A similar process can be used to reallocate assets between assets classes (i.e. equity to fixed-income or vice-versa), reallocate assets within assets classes (i.e. shifting from high duration/beta to low duration/beta), or pre-investing an anticipated inflow of cash.

Foreign Currency Risk

  • Transaction exposure
  • Translation exposure
  • Economic exposure
  • Foreign currency receipt
  • Foreign currency payment
Transaction exposure is the risk associated with a foreign exchange rate on a specific business transaction. Translation exposure is the risk associated with the conversion of foreign financial statements into domestic currency. Foreign currenct payments and receipts are exposed to risk associated with changes in the relative attractiveness of products and services offered for sale, arising out of the competitive effects of changes in exchange rates. In the case of a receipt, an investor can hedge by selling the currency forward and locking-in price. In the case of a payment, an investor can go long a currency forward. If an investor hedges against forward equity returns, a foreign asset portfolio will earn the foreign risk-free rate. If the investor also hedges the exchange rate, they will earn the domestic risk-free rate.
