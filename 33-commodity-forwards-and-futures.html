Commodity Forwards and Futures

Introduction

  • F0,T = S0e(r-q)T
  • Forward curve/forward strip
    • Contango
    • Backwardation
The forward curve is the set of prices for different expiration dates for a given commodity. Contango refers to an upward-sloping forward curve. Backwardation refers to a downward-sloping forward curve.

Equilibrium Pricing

  • Synthetic commodities
    • Long forward/long bond: ST - F0,T + F0,T = ST
  • F0,T = E0(ST)e(r-a)T
We can enter into a long forward contract and go long a zero-coupon bond with a payoff of the futures price in order to create a synthetic commodity. We can also derive the equation for the forward price which is the discountd expected spot price (where r is the risk-free rate and a is the commodity discount rate)

Lease Rate

Compensation required by an investor in exchange for lending a commodity (cost of borrowing)

  • F0,T = S0e(r-q)T
    • q = r - (1/T)ln(F0,T/S)
    • 1 = (1 + r)/(F0,T/S)1/T - 1
The lease rate is comparable to the dividend yield on financial assets but it isn't directly observable and must be estimated from the observed forward price.

Cash-and-Carry Arbitrage

Reverse Cash-and-Carry Arbitrage

Carry/Storage Costs

  • Sell today or sell tomorrow?
    • Compare PV of selling at time T with spot price
    • Indifference point: S0 = e-rT[F0,T - λ(0,T)]
  • F0,T ≥ S0erT + λ(0,T)
  • F0,T = S0e(r+λ)T + λ(0,T)
S0 = revenue from selling today; F - λ(0,T) = net revenue from selling at time T; λ(0,T) = future value of storage costs. Effectively, this is compensation for the cost of holding a commodity

Convenience Yield

Definition: Extra nonmonetary return that is generated by holding an asset

  • Business reasons for holding a commodity
  • No-arbitrage region
    • S0e(r+λ)T ≥ F0,T ≥ S0e(r+λ-c)T
For example, business reasons for holding a commodity may include insurance against a potential disruption in supply. In some cases, there may be no way for an investor to earn the convenience yield (they don't have a business reason for holding an asset and are unable to sell it to someone who does), thus leading to a no-arbitrage region rather than a single point.

Commodity Spreads

Commodities are often used as inputs in the creation of other commodities

  • Soybeans -> soybean meal/oil (crush spread)
  • Crude oil -> petroleum products (crack spread)

Hedging Strategies

  • Basis risk
  • Strip hedge
  • Stack hedge
Basis risk arises when the derivative contract used for hedging purposes does not exactly match what is being hedged (this could be due to differences in delivery location/date or grade of deliverable, among other variables). For example, airlines often use crude oil futures to hedge jet fuel prices. A strip hedge is created by matching individual obligations with a futures contract of matching maturity and quantity. A stack hedge is created by entering futures contracts with a single maturity structured to offset changes in the PV of future obligations. Combining a series of stack hedges is referred to as a stack-and-roll.
