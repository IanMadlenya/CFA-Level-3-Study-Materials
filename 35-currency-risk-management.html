Forward/Futures Hedging

  • V*t-V*0=VtSt-V0S0
  • Realized gain = V0(-Ft+F0)
  • Profit = VtSt-V0S0-V0(Ft-F0)
  • RH=(VtSt-V0S0)/(V0S0)-(Ft-F0)/S0=R*-RF
  • R*=R+s(1+R)
'Vt' is the value of the portfolio of foreign assets to hedge, measured in foreign currency at time t. 'V*t' is the value of the portfolio of foreign assets measured in domestic currency. 'St' is the spot exchange rate (domestic currency value of one unit of foreign currency quoted at time t. 'Ft' is the futures exchange rate (domestic currency value of one unit of foreign currency quoted at time t. 'R' is the rate of return of the portfolio measured in foreign currency terms, (Vt-V0)/V0. 'R*' is the rate of return of the portfolio measured in domestic currency terms, (V*t-V*0)/V*0. 's' is the percentage movement in the exchange, (St-S0)/S0.

Minimum Variance Hedge

  • RH=R*-hRF
  • h*=cov(R*,RF)/σ2F
  • R*=a+h*RF+ε
  • RH=R+s-hRF
  • RH=R+s(1-h)
  • h*=cov(R*,RF)/σ2F=cov(R+s,s)/σ2s=1+cov(R,s)/σ2s
'RH' is the rate of return on a hedged portfolio. 'h*' is the optimal hedge ratio, which minimizes the variance of 'RH'. In the final equation above, translation risk = 1 and economic risk = cov(R,s)/σ2s. Translation risk arises from the translation of the value of the asset from the foreign currency to the domestic currency. Economic risk arises when the foreign currency value of a foreign investment reacts systematically to an exchange rate movement (e.g. a drop in the value of the pound could lead to increases in the value of a British company due to boosts in exports). To hedge total currency risk, an investor must account for both translation and economic risk.

Basis Risk

  • Basis is equal to the interest rate differential between countries or the forward premium (discount) over the spot rate
  • Interest rate parity
    • F/S=(1+r$)/(1+£)
    • (F-S)/S=(r$-r£)/(1+r£)

Options

  • Insurance vs. hedge
  • Net dollar profit = V0(K-St)-V0P0, when K>St=-V0P0
  • Dynamic hedging
    • Options gain = n(Pt-P0)
    • Pt-P0=δ(St-S0)
    • Options gain = n(Pt-P0) = nδ(St-S0)
    • Portfolio gain = V0(St-S0)
    • Hedge ratio = -1/δ
Insurance exploits the asymmetric risk-return characteristics of an option while hedging takes into account the dymaics of the relationship between the option premium and the underlying exchange rate. Because the delta and hedge ratio vary with exchange rate, the number of options held must be adjusted continuously using the hedge ratio.

Strategic Currency Management

Long-run foreign currency exposure decision

  • Total portfolio risk
  • Asset types
  • Investment horizon
  • Structural/fundamental views
  • Costs
  • Regret aversion
Total portfolio risk accounts for diversification effects by considering foreign currency exposures in aggregate at the portfolio level rather than in isolation by investment. The correlation between different assets and currency movements varies. In theory, exchange rates should revert to the mean/fundamentals over long timer horizons.

Tactical Currency Management

Currency management is often delegated to a specialized manager or team using various approches

  • Currency risk profile management
  • Technical approach
  • Fundamental approach
The currency risk profile management approach assumes currency movements are unpredictable. It attempts to create asymmetric risk profiles that protect the downside while capturing the upside. The technical approach attempts to exploit market inefficiences within a controlled risk management program. The fundamental appraoch employes economic analysis to detect under- or overvalued currencies.
