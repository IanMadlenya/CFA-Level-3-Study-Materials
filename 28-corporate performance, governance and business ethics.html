Stakeholders and the Enterprise

A company's stakeholders are those individuals or groups with an interest, claim, or stake in the company and it actions

Stakeholder Impact Analysis

Definition: The process of identifying the most important stakeholders and giving highest priority to pursuing strategies that satisfy their needs

  • Steps:
    1. Identify stakeholders
    2. Identify stakehoders' interests and concerns
    3. Identify what claims stakeholders are likely to make on the organization
    4. Identify stakeholders who are most important from the organization's perspective
    5. Identify resulting strategic challenges

The Unique Role of Stockholders

  • Stockholders are legal owners and the providers of risk capital (a major source of capital that allows a company to operate its business)
  • No guarantee they will recoup their investment or earn a return
  • Managers typically seek to maximize returns that stockholders receive

Agency Theory

Definition: Examines the problems that can arise in a business relationship when one person delegates decision-making authority to another

  • Principal-agent relationship
    • Arises when one party delegates decision-making authroity or control of resources to another
  • The agency problem
    • Arises when agents and principals have different goals and if agents take actions that are not in the best interests of their principals
    • Enabled by the fact that agents normally have more information than principals (information asymmetry)

Ethics and Strategy

  • Preeminent goal of managers is to pursue strategies that maximize long-term profitability and growth in order to boost returns to stockholders
  • However, actions should be consistent with applicable laws and ethics
  • Common causes of unethical behavior:
    1. Flawed personal ethics among managers
    2. Neglecting to consider ethical implications of decisions in favor of straightforward economic calculus
    3. Organizational cultures that deemphasize ethics
    4. Pressure to meet unrealistic performance goals
    5. Unethical leadership

Ethics and Philosophy

  • The Friedman doctrine - businesses' only social responsibility is the pursuit of profit, subject to the constraint that the engage in free and open competition
  • Utilitarian ethics - the moral worth of actions is determined by their consequences
  • Kantian ethics - people should be treated as ends and nevers purely as means to the ends of others
  • Rights theories - humans have fundamental rights and priviliges that establish a minimum level of morally acceptable behavior
  • Justice theories - focus on the attainment of a just distribution of economic goods and services

Ensuring Basic Ethical Principles

  1. Favor hiring and promoting people with a well-grounded sense of personal ethics
  2. Build an organizational culture that places a high value on ethical behavior
  3. Ensure that leaders within the business not only articulate the principles of ethical behavior but also act in a manner consistent with their words
  4. Establish decision-making processes that require people to consider the ethical dimension of decisions
  5. Hire ethics officers
  6. Establish strong governance processes
  7. Act with moral courage
